Ilford revisited in 2010
by John Winsor, December 2010.		 HOME


Having left Ilford in 1958 a visit there today is an astounding experience, something like a trip in a time machine.  I fantasise that some Pied Piper had walked through the streets, and all the English came streaming out of their houses and followed him over the horizon.  And when the town was empty and deserted, vast hoards of Asians from the sub-continent came pouring in.  They occupied the abandoned homes, schools and offices.  They slept in the English beds and took over the English lives.  A walk down Ilford High Road today is like a trip to Mumbai.

I want to say, though, that the Asians have done better than the English ever did.  They installed double glazing and central heating.  They ripped-out front gardens and paved them over to accommodate their enormous people carriers.  They have lots of money and they are busy spending it.

Interestingly, examination of Google satellite images reveals that, in many cases, individual homeowners have had a cottage constructed at the far end of their back gardens.  The purpose, no doubt, was to provide independent accommodation for their ageing parents, something unknown to the original ethnic English owners.  Though I would say that, in my time, most of the original owners would have lacked the financial resources to do this.

The streets where I grew up are quiet, the homes neat and well kept.  There is, of course, the occasional Islamic Learning Centre (a mosque by any other name...), and the occasional outrageous Romanesque plaster portico.  How could the Council give approval for that?  Well, actually, the Council are all Indian too.

In the High Road nothing remains of the building that was once the Handley fish shop.  It was demolished and rebuilt long ago.  But many other shops retain their original Victorian brickwork and windows, still evident above the glass-and-chrome shopfronts.

Occasionally I see an ethnic English person walking along.  I feel like calling out:  "Hey, we're both English!  Isn't that something!"  I have lunch in a café.  The staff are friendly and condescend to speak English.  But they look at me with curious eyes.

Ilford today is multi-cultural only to the extent that it comprises Hindus, Muslims and Sikhs.  Anglo-Saxons are not part of the mix.  As did the Saxons once banish the Celts, so in turn have they been displaced by cultures better adapted to survive.  The question is how to talk about these matters without risking the accusation of xenophobia?

But it might be said that the the ethnic English had only themselves to blame.  Faced with the government's policy of encouraging mass immigration without adequate planning, nobody forced the English to sell-up and move away.  Evidently they regarded themselves as entirely incompatible with the newcomers and their Indian culture.  Might the Ilford English have been victims of their own prejudice?


Revisited again in 2011
by John Winsor, July 2011.

Another pilgrimage to Ilford, and I have lunch in Bodgers' cafeteria.  Bodgers must be among the last of the traditional English department stores, dating from 1921.  Gangan would have known it well.

I find the cafeteria clientele exclusively to comprise elderly English ladies taking tea.  This is their little oasis.  I would expect to find the same kind of people in the tearoom of Raffles Hotel in Singapore, assuming that Raffles now caters for elderly ex-patriots.  It is as though there were an invisible notice that said:  "Whites only".

Have Bodgers and Raffles come to symbolize the last vestiges of a now defunct English presence?  Has Ilford come to resemble an ex-British colony, now returned to its "rightful" owners?


Revisited yet again in 2012
by John Winsor, Summer 2012.

Still seeking the Holy Grail of a continuing ethnic English residency in the borough.  Surely it must be there somewhere, perhaps clinging onto existence in the posh and leafy outskirts?

Not that anywhere in Ilford can truly be described as "posh".  The newest and best district, constructed in the late 1930s, is to be found in Barkingside which is pressed hard against the green belt.

So I take the bus to Barkingside to find my old school.  It looks surprisingly the same as it did sixty years ago.  How I would love to be ushered inside.

But an inspection of the school's external noticeboard reveals that the revolution has been complete.  The names both of staff and of prominent students say everything.  Do my feelings of regret betray a dormant racism?  I don't think so.  It's just a sentimental regard for times past.

When did the Ilford English ever vote for political policies that would change the race, culture and religion of their world?  When did they ever ask to be transformed into strangers in their own town, and feel obliged to move away?
